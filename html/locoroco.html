MY ROLE

During the research phase, I focused on the process and difficulties of becoming a street vendor and mapped out the relationship between the vendors and authorities. In the design process, we produced the handbook together. I identified the personas of target users and worked on the kit design with Yihan. I modeled and rendered the design in Twinmotion and created demonstrative videos. Daniel came up with the application’s wireframe and prototype. I also coded the website using HTML.

BACKGROUND

Street vending has been an important part of cities and their economy for a long time worldwide. It provides working opportunities for vendors and increases merchant selection for consumers. However, the impression of street vendors is not always positive; some people view them as disruptors of city order because of their uncleanness and mobility. Vendors are facing various difficulties and pressures from society and City authorities. Many of them are immigrants who cannot speak English fluently while their whole family depends on this low-income business on the street. Robbery, harassment, and police enforcement are also common for street vendors. In New York City, the process of becoming a vendor consumes highly on time and money.

OBJECTIVE

LocoRoco Vending Project aims to help vendors achieve solidarity by setting up a platform for individuals to help each other by exchanging ideas, providing delivery services, and sharing resources. It develops two major parts, a physical kit and a virtual platform for the community. The physical kit consists of a portable WIFI service and operable panels for the vendor's meeting space construction. This kit aims to create internal connections among vendors by allowing them to assemble meetings and form spaces whenever is needed. The mobile app features vendor mapping and delivery service for vendors and consumers and a platform for vendors and restaurant owners to cooperate.

Lower Manhattan Street Vending System

The current street vending system in New York City comprises different types of vendors, the city authorities, a nonprofit organization -- the Street Vending Project, parks and public restrooms, vending cart companies, commissaries, restaurants and markets, consumers, and delivery platforms. General vendors and food vendors are under administrative control by the authorities. The Department of Consumer Affairs legislates restricted streets for general vendors and food vendors. The Department of Park and Recreation controls those who vend in parks and have limited access to public restrooms. Other authorities in New York City are responsible for making regulations,  enforcement, issuing license, permit, and certificate for vendors while being supervised by City Hall and City Council. The non-profit Street Vendor Project represents vendors and helps them negotiate with authorities. Vending cart companies and commissaries provide carts and cart storage space for vendors and regularly examine city authorities. Restaurants and markets sometimes cooperate with street vendors for raw materials. There are three main types of consumers: students, office workers, and tourists in the city.  

​

​All purchases from street vendors, restaurants, and markets regularly create a rivalry relationship among those three entrepreneurs. With the development of delivery and online paying platforms, restaurants can extend their consumer group. Additionally, because of the COVID-19 pandemic, the City allows outdoor dining that takes up street vending space for restaurants. All these lead to higher stress levels for vendors.

PROPOSED SYSTEM

The LocoRoco Vending Project proposes a new system that vendors in clusters will form to reduce stress and fear from vendors and create solidarity in the community. Taking the lower Manhattan area as an example, several gathering spots function as centers of the clusters. The vendors surrounding one gathering spot form a cluster to develop a cooperative relationship without hierarchies. The clusters create collaborative relationships with other facilities, especially cooperates and shares information with the existing Street Vendor Project.

 

​

The project aims to order carts from retail shops in New York City, so vendors can buy carts from the organization directly without going to shops far from them and bring the carts back to their locations. It also helps vendors find proper commissaries and assists them in moving and cleaning the carts. It will communicate with shops and restaurants to provide long-term restrooms for vendors. A new mobile app will be developed for vendors to use online payments, delivery, and advertisements.

PHYSICAL KIT

The physical kit consists of a box with portable WIFI and canvas panels that can be assembled into chairs, tables, and screens. The panels are connected by aluminum tubes that allow them to rotate and be fixed at different angles. When the panels are not in use, vendors can collect the panels inside the kit, and assemble them when needed. In the weekly gatherings, vendors can use their carts and the kits to form a meeting space.

MOBILE APP

Launch Screen

Vendor Home Screen

Vendors can find the latest policies and laws on their home page. They can also find restaurants and commissaries willing to cooperate with them and contact them via the APP. 

Consumer Home Screen

Consumers can find the nearest and popular street vendors on their home screens. If they want, they can order the food as takeouts. 

Street Vendor Shopping Page

Street Vendors can find the highest-rated vending carts from their shopping screen. By comparing other vendors' reviews, one can make proper cart purchases that can be sent directly to the commissary. 

Map

On the map page, vendors can look up restrooms, meeting spots, and other facilities.
